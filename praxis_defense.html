Introduction: The Problem with Traditional IDS

As cyberattacks become more sophisticated, traditional Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and manual machine learning approaches are failing to keep pace.

The Old Way: Manual & Slow

Manual Feature Engineering

Time-consuming, requires rare expertise, and prone to human bias.

Trial-and-Error Model Selection

Inefficient search for the right algorithm for a specific problem.

Laborious Hyperparameter Tuning

Manual adjustments often lead to suboptimal model performance.

The Consequences

High False Positives

Overwhelms security analysts with false alarms, causing alert fatigue.

Missed Novel Threats

Static, signature-based systems are easily bypassed by new attack vectors.

Inability to Scale

Manual processes cannot keep up with the volume and complexity of modern data.

Positioning my work within the current state of research.

Paper Contribution Gaps Identified
Pinto et al. (2023) Systematic review (2018-2022) of 98 papers on ML/AI IDS for ICS/SCADA and IIoT. Confirmed trade-off between missing new attacks (signature) and high false positives (anomaly). Survey only; does not experimentally compare algorithms; fails to resolve accuracy vs. false-alarm dilemma.
Sezgin & Boyacı (2023) Developed AID4I Automl Framework viability on IIoT. Proved automation improves accuracy by up to 14.39% and reduces training time. Relied on a single AutoML pipeline; did not benchmark across platforms.
Liu et al. (2022) Audited CSE-CIC-IDS2018 dataset by reverse-engineering the closed-source labelling logic, reconstructing clean versions, and measuring corruption levels, finding a 7.53% label error rate. Provided a corrected dataset. Nuances in Attempted and Infiltration attack types. Justifies my use of an "improved" dataset, ensuring validity.
Darko (2023) Proposes a BERT-style Transformer that learns directly from raw flows; fine-tuned on 6400 labelled CIC-IDS-2018 records, achieving 99.82% accuracy with auto feature selection. Reviewed a single architecture approach and requires manual fine tuning. Strong benchmark, but FPR of 0.1% can still be too high for practical operations.

A Foundation of Truth: The Dataset Dilemma

This research addresses a critical flaw in many benchmark studies by building its models on a foundation of verified, high-integrity data.

The Problem: Corrupted Benchmarks

The original CSE-CIC-IDS2018 dataset, used in many studies, has been found to have significant flaws:

  • 7.53% of the entire dataset is mislabeled.
  • Some attack classes have significant label corruption.

Our Solution: Verified Data

This praxis exclusively uses the corrected and validated version of the dataset provided by Liu et al. (2022).

This ensures our models learn from real attack patterns, not data artifacts, making the results more robust and reliable.

Methodology

An End-to-End Automated Pipeline

Phase 1: Data Preparation

1. Data Curation & Partitioning

Started with the corrected CSE-CIC-IDS2018 dataset, which was cleaned, deduplicated, and partitioned into training (80%), validation (8%), and test (12%) sets.

2. Hybrid Resampling & Subsetting

Applied a hybrid resampling strategy (SMOTE, SMOTE-ENN, Undersampling) to the training data to reduce class imbalance from ~370k:1 to ~72:1, then split it into two training subsets.

Phase 2: AutoML Execution & Validation

Training & Evaluation

Used 2 subsets to train 6 models on 3 platforms (AWS, Azure, AutoGluon), evaluated on unseen test data.
